1 Topic & Definition 🔴 VIP — Highest Weightage Chapter

Topic: Accounting Ratios are a key tool of financial statement analysis, used to assess the liquidity, solvency, efficiency and profitability of an enterprise.

Definition: A ratio is a mathematical number expressing the relationship between two or more numbers — as a fraction, proportion, percentage, or number of times. When calculated from financial statement figures, it is an accounting ratio.

Golden Rule: A ratio is meaningful only when the two numbers are logically related — e.g. Furniture vs Purchases gives a number (a ratio value) but has no analytical relevance.

Likely Q: "What is an accounting ratio? Why must the two variables used be logically related?"

3 Objectives of Ratio Analysis 🟡 Conceptual

  • Identify areas needing more attention
  • Identify potential areas for improvement
  • Provide deeper analysis of profitability, liquidity, solvency, efficiency
  • Enable cross-sectional analysis vs industry standards
  • Support projections/estimates for the future
Likely Q: "What are the objectives of ratio analysis?"

4 Advantages of Ratio Analysis 🟠 Frequently Asked

  1. Helps understand efficacy of decisions
  2. Simplifies complex figures & shows relationships
  3. Helpful in comparative analysis (trend over years)
  4. Identification of problem areas
  5. Enables SWOT analysis
  6. Various comparisons: Intra-firm (time series), Inter-firm (cross-sectional), vs Industry standard

5 Limitations of Ratio Analysis 🔴 VIP

Two sub-groups: limitations inherited from financial statements, and limitations of ratios themselves.

From Financial Statements (5)

  • Limitations of accounting data (judgement-based)
  • Ignores price-level changes
  • Ignores qualitative/non-monetary aspects
  • Variations in accounting practices (hurts comparability)
  • Forecasting purely from history isn't reliable

Of Ratios Themselves (5)

  • Means, not the end
  • Lack ability to resolve problems (only indicative)
  • Lack of standardised definitions (e.g. "liquid liabilities")
  • Lack of universally accepted ideal/standard levels
  • Ratios based on unrelated figures are meaningless
Likely Q: "State any five limitations of ratio analysis." (Very frequently asked)

7 Liquidity Ratios 🔴 VIP — Guaranteed Numerical

1. Current Ratio ▶
Current Ratio = Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities

Ideal: 2:1. Includes: Current investments, Inventories, Trade receivables, Cash & equivalents, ST loans/advances, other current assets (prepaid exp., advance tax) ÷ ST borrowings, Trade payables, Other current liabilities, ST provisions.

2. Quick (Liquid / Acid-Test) Ratio ▶
Quick Ratio = Quick Assets ÷ Current Liabilities

Quick Assets = Current Assets − (Inventories + Prepaid Expenses + Advance Tax). Ideal: 1:1. Also called Acid-Test Ratio — excludes least liquid current assets.

Likely Q: "Calculate Current Ratio and Quick Ratio from the given Balance Sheet." (Guaranteed numerical, every year)

8 Solvency Ratios 🔴 VIP — Guaranteed Numerical

1. Debt-Equity Ratio ▶
Debt-Equity Ratio = Long-term Debts ÷ Shareholders' Funds

Shareholders' Funds = Share Capital + Reserves & Surplus + Money received against Share Warrants (= Non-current Assets + Working Capital − Non-current Liabilities). Ideal/safe: 2:1 (varies by industry).

2. Debt to Capital Employed Ratio ▶
= Long-term Debt ÷ Capital Employed (or Net Assets)

Capital Employed = Long-term Debt + Shareholders' Funds = Total Assets − Current Liabilities.

3. Proprietary Ratio ▶
= Shareholders' Funds ÷ Capital Employed (or Total Assets)

Key relation: Debt to Capital Employed Ratio + Proprietary Ratio = 1 (i.e. 100%).

4. Total Assets to Debt Ratio ▶
= Total Assets ÷ Long-term Debts

Higher ratio = better coverage of debt by assets. Its reciprocal (using net assets) = Debt to Capital Employed Ratio.

5. Interest Coverage Ratio ▶
= Net Profit before Interest & Tax ÷ Interest on Long-term Debts

Reveals how many times interest is covered by available profit — higher = safer for lenders.

Likely Q: "Calculate Debt-Equity Ratio, Proprietary Ratio and Total Assets to Debt Ratio from the given Balance Sheet." (Multi-part numerical, very common)

9 Activity (Turnover) Ratios 🔴 VIP — Guaranteed Numerical

1. Inventory Turnover Ratio ▶
= Cost of Revenue from Operations ÷ Average Inventory

Average Inventory = (Opening + Closing Inventory) ÷ 2. Low = bad buying/obsolete stock; too high = risk of stockouts.

2. Trade Receivables Turnover Ratio ▶
= Net Credit Revenue from Operations ÷ Average Trade Receivables
Average Collection Period = 365 (or 12) ÷ Trade Receivables Turnover Ratio
3. Trade Payables Turnover Ratio ▶
= Net Credit Purchases ÷ Average Trade Payables
Average Payment Period = 365 (or 12) ÷ Trade Payables Turnover Ratio
4. Net Assets (Capital Employed) Turnover Ratio ▶
= Revenue from Operations ÷ Capital Employed
5. Fixed Assets Turnover Ratio ▶
= Net Revenue from Operations ÷ Net Fixed Assets
6. Working Capital Turnover Ratio ▶
= Net Revenue from Operations ÷ Working Capital

Higher turnover of capital employed/fixed assets/working capital = better utilisation of resources.

Likely Q: "Calculate Inventory Turnover Ratio and Trade Receivables Turnover Ratio, along with the Average Collection Period." (Classic multi-part numerical)

12 Memory Tricks & Mnemonics 🟢 Quick Revision

4 Categories: "LSAP" — Liquidity, Solvency, Activity, Profitability.
Liquidity (2): "Current comes first, Quick follows" — Current Ratio (all CA/CL), Quick Ratio (excludes inventory + prepaid + advance tax).
Solvency (5): "Debt Dances Proudly To Interest" — Debt-Equity, Debt to Capital Employed, Proprietary, Total assets to debt, Interest coverage.
Activity (6): "I Trade Trade, Net Fixed Working" — Inventory, Trade receivables, Trade payables, Net assets, Fixed assets, Working capital turnover.
Profitability (10): "GO-ON-Bring Really Excellent Dividend Prices" (see section 10).
Proprietary + Debt-to-CE = 1: Remember this relationship saves a full calculation in exams.

13 One-Page Revision Sheet 🟢 Quick Revision

4 Categories (LSAP)

  • Liquidity (2) → Solvency (5) → Activity (6) → Profitability (10)

Liquidity

  • Current = CA/CL (2:1); Quick = (CA−Inventory−Prepaid−AdvTax)/CL (1:1)

Solvency

  • Debt-Equity = LT Debt/SH Funds; Proprietary = SH Funds/Capital Employed; sum of Debt-to-CE + Proprietary = 1

Activity

  • Inventory TO = COGS/Avg Inventory; Receivables TO = Credit Sales/Avg Receivables; Collection period = 365/TO ratio

Profitability

  • GP% = GP/Sales×100; NP% = NP/Sales×100; ROI = PBIT/Capital Employed×100; EPS = (PAT−Pref Div)/No. of Equity Shares

Key Benchmarks

  • Current 2:1 | Quick 1:1 | Debt-Equity 2:1 (industry-dependent)

Limitations — 2 Groups of 5

  • From FS: judgement-bias, price-level, non-monetary, accounting variation, poor forecasting
  • Of ratios: means-not-end, no problem-solving, no standard definitions, no universal ideal levels, unrelated-figure risk

14 Last-Day Revision Notes 🟢 Quick Revision

✓ Ratio = mathematical relationship between two logically-connected numbers.
✓ Functional classification: Liquidity → Solvency → Activity → Profitability (LSAP).
✓ Current Ratio ideal = 2:1; Quick Ratio ideal = 1:1.
✓ Quick Assets = Current Assets − Inventories − Prepaid Expenses − Advance Tax.
✓ Debt-Equity Ratio = Long-term Debt ÷ Shareholders' Funds; safe level ~2:1.
✓ Proprietary Ratio + Debt to Capital Employed Ratio = 1 (always).
✓ Interest Coverage Ratio = PBIT ÷ Interest on long-term debt.
✓ Inventory Turnover = Cost of Revenue from Operations ÷ Average Inventory.
✓ Average Collection/Payment Period = 365 ÷ respective Turnover Ratio.
✓ ROI (ROCE) uses Profit BEFORE Interest and Tax (PBIT); RONW uses Profit AFTER Tax.
✓ EPS = (Profit after Tax − Preference Dividend) ÷ Number of Equity Shares.
✓ P/E Ratio = Market Price per Share ÷ EPS.
