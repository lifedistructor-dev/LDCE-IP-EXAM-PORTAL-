7.1

Meaning of Depreciation

🔴 Very Important for Exam
Depreciation: a permanent, continuing and gradual decline in the book value of a fixed (tangible) asset due to use, passage of time, or obsolescence — based on cost, not market value. AS-6 (ICAI): "a measure of the wearing out, consumption or other loss of value of a depreciable asset arising from use, effluxion of time or obsolescence through technology and market change."
📝 Probable Exam QuestionDefine depreciation. Why is it charged even though the asset may still be usable?

7.3

Causes of Depreciation

🟠 Frequently Asked
Causes of Depreciation
Wear & Tear
  • Due to use or passage of time
Expiration of Legal Rights
  • Patents, copyrights, leases end
Obsolescence
  • Tech change, better alternatives, demand shift
Abnormal Factors
  • Fire, earthquake, floods — sudden not gradual
📝 Probable Exam QuestionState any four causes of depreciation.
7.4

Need for Depreciation

🟠 Frequently Asked
  • Matching of Costs & Revenue — depreciation is a cost like any other expense, must be charged against the revenue it helped earn
  • Tax Consideration — depreciation is a deductible cost for tax purposes
  • True & Fair Financial Position — without it, assets are overvalued, distorting the Balance Sheet
  • Compliance with Law — corporate enterprises are legally required to provide for depreciation

7.6

Straight Line Method (SLM) & Written Down Value (WDV)

🔴 Guaranteed numerical every year

Straight Line Method

ANNUAL DEPRECIATION (SLM)(Cost − Scrap Value) ÷ Estimated Useful Life
RATE OF DEPRECIATION(Annual Depreciation ÷ Acquisition Cost) × 100

Example: Cost ₹2,50,000, Scrap ₹50,000, Life 10 yrs → Depreciation = ₹20,000/year (fixed) → Rate = 8%

Written Down Value (Reducing Balance) Method

ANNUAL DEPRECIATION (WDV)Book Value at start of year × Rate %
RATE OF DEPRECIATIONR = [1 − ⁿ√(Scrap Value ÷ Cost)] × 100

Example: Cost ₹2,00,000 @10% WDV → Yr1 Dep. ₹20,000 (WDV ₹1,80,000) → Yr2 Dep. ₹18,000 (WDV ₹1,62,000) — declining every year

📝 Probable Exam QuestionCalculate depreciation for the first three years using both SLM and WDV methods, given cost, scrap value and useful life. (Compulsory numerical)

7.11

Provisions

🔴 Very Important for Exam
Provision: an amount set aside, by charge against profit, for a known expense/loss of the current period whose exact amount is uncertain (e.g. Provision for Doubtful Debts).

7.12

Reserves

🔴 Very Important — Provision vs Reserve is a guaranteed question
Reserve: an appropriation of profit (not a charge) retained in the business to strengthen its financial position or meet future needs — shown in "Reserves & Surplus" after Capital.

Provision vs Reserve

ProvisionReserve
Charge against profitAppropriation of profit
For a known liability/expense, uncertain amountTo strengthen financial position; some mandatory by law
Reduces taxable profitNo effect on taxable profit (created after tax)
Shown as deduction from asset, or with current liabilitiesShown on liabilities side after Capital
Compulsory — even without profitsDiscretionary (mostly); cannot be created without profits
Cannot be used for dividendGeneral reserve CAN be used for dividend

Types of Reserves

Reserves
By Purpose
  • General — free, any purpose
  • Specific — Dividend Equalisation, Workmen Compensation, Investment Fluctuation, Debenture Redemption
By Source
  • Revenue Reserve — from normal trading profit; dividend-eligible
  • Capital Reserve — from capital profit (share premium, profit on asset sale); NOT dividend-eligible
📝 Probable Exam QuestionDistinguish between Provision and Reserve; and between Revenue Reserve and Capital Reserve.
7.13

Secret Reserve

🟠 Frequently Asked
Secret Reserve: a reserve that does NOT appear in the Balance Sheet — reduces disclosed profit and tax liability; can be merged back into profits during lean periods.
Created by:
  • Charging excessive/higher depreciation than required
  • Undervaluation of inventory/stock
  • Charging capital expenditure to Profit & Loss A/c
  • Making excessive provision for doubtful debts
  • Showing contingent liabilities as actual liabilities

Key Terms Glossary — Chapter 7

🔴 Quick-fire revision

Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

🟢 Quick Revision
D‑D‑A = Tangible, Natural, Intangible
Depreciation (tangible assets), Depletion (natural resources), Amortisation (intangible assets) — three cousins, three asset families.
W‑E‑O‑A
Causes of depreciation → Wear & tear, Expiration of legal rights, Obsolescence, Abnormal factors.
SLM = Same every year; WDV = Weakens (declines) every year
Straight Line → fixed depreciation charge. Written Down Value → charge keeps shrinking as the base (book value) shrinks.
Provision = "Profit ka charge"; Reserve = "Ruk ke appropriate"
Provision is deducted BEFORE net profit is known (a charge); Reserve is set aside AFTER net profit is known (an appropriation).
"Secret reserves hide in plain sight"
Secret reserve never appears in the Balance Sheet — created via excess depreciation, undervalued stock, or excess provisions.

Last-Day Revision Notes

🔴 Read this 30 minutes before the exam
  1. 01Depreciation is based on COST, not market value — even if market value rises.
  2. 02Depletion = natural resources; Amortisation = intangible assets; Depreciation = tangible fixed assets.
  3. 03SLM: fixed depreciation every year, based on original cost. NOT recognised by Income Tax Law.
  4. 04WDV: declining depreciation every year, based on book value. IS recognised by Income Tax Law.
  5. 05WDV value can never reach exactly zero; SLM can be written down to scrap/zero.
  6. 06Provision = charge against profit (before net profit is known); Reserve = appropriation (after net profit is known).
  7. 07Provision reduces taxable profit; Reserve has no effect on taxable profit.
  8. 08General Reserve can fund dividend distribution; a Provision never can.
  9. 09Revenue Reserve → dividend-eligible; Capital Reserve → NOT dividend-eligible (from capital profits like share premium).
  10. 10Secret Reserve never appears in the Balance Sheet — created via excess depreciation/provisions or undervalued stock.
