11.1Meaning & Features

ConceptualFrequently Asked
Definition: Accounting records not kept strictly per double entry system. Often (wrongly) called "Single Entry System" — there is no such actual system; it is an unsystematic mix where cash and personal accounts are properly maintained but assets/liabilities/revenue/expenses are only partially recorded.
🏷 6 Features of Incomplete Records
1. Unsystematic method of recording
2. Cash & personal accounts maintained properly; revenue/expense/asset/liability info missing
3. Owner's personal transactions may get mixed into the cash book
4. No uniformity across organisations — records not comparable
5. Heavy dependence on original vouchers (invoices) to reconstruct figures
6. Profit/loss can only be estimated, not ascertained with full accuracy
🎯 Probable Question: "State any four features/limitations of incomplete records." (3–4 marks, theory — very common opener question)

11.2Reasons & Limitations

Quick Revision

✅ Why Businesses Adopt It

  • No need for accounting knowledge
  • Inexpensive — no specialised accountant
  • Less time consuming
  • Convenient — owner records only what matters to them

⚠️ Limitations

  • Unreliable, unverifiable figures
  • True profit/loss cannot be ascertained
  • Omissions go undetected
  • Not acceptable as-is for tax/loan purposes

11.3Statement of Affairs Method

Very ImportantFrequently Asked
Two statements — opening & closing — list assets on one side, liabilities on the other. The balancing figure = Capital. Also called the Net Worth Method.
Format — Statement of Affairs
LiabilitiesAmtAssetsAmt
Bills payable, Creditors, O/S expenses…Land, Machinery, Furniture, Stock…
Capital (balancing figure)xxxDebtors, Cash/Bank, Prepaid exp.xxx
Note: If liabilities side total exceeds assets side, Capital shows on the ASSETS side — representing a debit balance (negative capital).

Master Formula — Profit or Loss

Capital at End
↓
+ Drawings during year
↓
− Additional Capital introduced
↓
− Capital at Beginning
↓
= Profit (or Loss if negative)
🎯 Probable Question: Full numerical — given opening/closing assets & liabilities, drawings and capital introduced, "calculate profit using statement of affairs method." (6–8 marks — appears almost every year)
Remember: "CDAC" — Closing Capital + Drawings − Additional Capital − Opening Capital = Profit.

11.4Conversion Method

Very Important
Reconstructing proper Trading & P&L A/c and Balance Sheet by putting incomplete records "in order" — i.e., deriving the missing figures using double-entry logic.
🔍 8 Commonly Missing Items
Opening capital · Credit purchases · Credit sales · Bills payable accepted · Bills receivable received · Payments to creditors · Payments to debtors · Other cash/bank items

11.4.1Ascertaining Credit Purchases

Very Important
Prepare a Total Creditors Account; Credit Purchases is the balancing figure.
Total Creditors Account (Format)
Dr. — ParticularsAmtCr. — ParticularsAmt
Cash/Bank paid, Bills payable accepted, Discount received, Purchases return…Balance b/d, Bank (cheque dishonoured), Bills payable dishonoured…
Balance c/d…Credit Purchases (balancing figure)xxx
🎯 Worked example: Opening creditors 40,000 + Credit purchases (bal. fig.) 97,000 − Cash paid 85,000 − Discount 2,000 = Closing creditors 50,000.

11.4.2Ascertainment of Credit Sales

Very Important
Prepare a Total Debtors Account; Credit Sales is the balancing figure. Deduct Sales Return from gross credit sales to get net credit sales.

11.4.3Bills Receivable & Bills Payable

Frequently Asked
When bills received/accepted during the year is missing, prepare Total Bills Receivable A/c and Total Bills Payable A/c — balancing figure gives the missing item.

📥 Total Bills Receivable A/c

Dr: Balance b/d + Sundry debtors (bills received)
Cr: Bank (bills honoured) + Sundry debtors (dishonoured) + Balance c/d

📤 Total Bills Payable A/c

Dr: Bank (bills matured) + Sundry creditors (dishonoured) + Balance c/d
Cr: Balance b/d + Sundry creditors (bills accepted)

🎯 Probable Question: Numerical combining both Bills R/P accounts to find "bills received/accepted during the year" — 4 marks, moderate frequency.

🚨 Last-Day Revision Notes

  • "Single Entry System" is a misnomer — no separate accounting system by that name actually exists.
  • Opening capital always comes from the Statement of Affairs at the START of the year.
  • Drawings ADD BACK to closing capital; additional capital introduced is SUBTRACTED — don't reverse the signs.
  • Sales return is deducted AFTER finding credit sales as the balancing figure, not before.
  • If liabilities exceed assets in a Statement of Affairs, capital appears on the assets side (debit balance).
Quick Trick: Whichever account's missing figure you need — Creditors → Purchases, Debtors → Sales, Bills → Bills received/accepted — always put it as the BALANCING figure.
