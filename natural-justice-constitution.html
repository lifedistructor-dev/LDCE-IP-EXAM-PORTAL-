Art. 32: "Heart and soul of the Constitution" (Dr. Ambedkar) — right to move Supreme Court for enforcement of Fundamental Rights; 5 writs: Habeas Corpus, Mandamus, Prohibition, Quo Warranto, Certiorari
Art. 226: High Court writ power — WIDER than Art. 32 (for FR enforcement + "any other purpose")
Art. 226(3): Interim order vacation application must be disposed within 2 weeks
Art. 227: High Court's power of superintendence over all courts/tribunals in its territory (except Armed Forces tribunals)
Art. 311: Civil servant dismissal/removal safeguards — cannot be dismissed by authority subordinate to appointing authority; needs inquiry + reasonable opportunity of hearing
Art. 311(2) exceptions (no inquiry needed): (a) conviction on criminal charge, (b) not reasonably practicable, (c) security of State

🌳 Principles of Natural Justice — Mind Map प्राकृतिक न्याय के सिद्धांत
Principles of Natural Justice
1. Audi Alteram Partem — "Hear the other side": right to notice, right to present case, right to know evidence against oneself, right to cross-examine, right to legal representation (in serious cases)
2. Nemo Judex in Causa Sua — "No one shall be a judge in his own cause": Rule against Bias — pecuniary bias, personal bias, subject-matter bias, departmental bias
3. Reasoned Decision / Speaking Order — decisions must state reasons (judicially recognised as part of natural justice; linked to Art. 14 non-arbitrariness)

Constitutional Anchoring 🔴

Natural justice is not separately "written" in the Constitution but is read into Art. 14 (non-arbitrariness), Art. 21 (fair procedure), and Art. 311(2) (reasonable opportunity of hearing) by judicial interpretation — most famously in Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978), which held Art. 21's "procedure" must be fair, just & reasonable.

Probable Q: Name the two fundamental principles of natural justice.
Answer: Audi Alteram Partem (hear the other side) and Nemo Judex in Causa Sua (rule against bias).

Probable Q: Which amendment inserted "Socialist" and "Secular" into the Preamble, and when did it take effect?
Answer: 42nd Amendment Act, 1976, effective 3 January 1977.
🔴 Article 14 — Equality Before Law Art. 14▶

Text

"The State shall not deny to any person equality before the law or the equal protection of the laws within the territory of India."

ConceptOriginMeaning
Equality before lawBritish (negative concept)No special privileges; all subject to ordinary law
Equal protection of lawsAmerican (positive concept)Equals treated equally; permits reasonable classification

Twin Test for Valid Classification 🔴

(i) Intelligible differentia distinguishing grouped persons from others; (ii) Rational nexus between the differentia and the object sought to be achieved.

Art. 14 is the constitutional root of the rule against arbitrariness — connects directly to natural justice, since an arbitrary (unheard/unreasoned) decision violates Art. 14.

Probable Q: What is the "twin test" for a valid classification under Article 14?
Answer: Intelligible differentia + rational nexus with the object of the law.
🔴 Article 21 & 21A — Life, Personal Liberty & Education Art. 21-21A▶

Article 21 — Text

"No person shall be deprived of his life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law."

Pre-1978: "Procedure established by law" = any enacted procedure (A.K. Gopalan case)
↓ Maneka Gandhi v. UOI (1978) 🔴
Procedure must be FAIR, JUST & REASONABLE (not arbitrary)

Article 21A 🟢

Free & compulsory education for children aged 6 to 14 years — inserted by the 86th Amendment Act, 2002 (implemented via RTE Act, 2009).

Judicial expansion: Art. 21 now covers right to livelihood, dignity, privacy, clean environment, speedy trial, legal aid, etc. — all read via natural justice reasoning.

Probable Q: Which landmark case held that "procedure established by law" under Article 21 must be fair, just and reasonable?
Answer: Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978).
🔴 Article 22 — Protection Against Arrest & Detention Art. 22▶

Art. 22(1) & (2): Ordinary Arrest Rights 🔴

• Informed of grounds of arrest "as soon as may be"
• Right to consult and be defended by a legal practitioner of choice
• Produced before nearest magistrate within 24 hours of arrest (excluding journey time)
• No detention beyond 24 hours without magistrate's authority

Art. 22(3): Exceptions — Rights DON'T Apply To 🟠

(a) Enemy alien; (b) Person arrested/detained under a preventive detention law.

Art. 22(4)-(7): Preventive Detention Safeguards

Detention beyond 3 months needs an Advisory Board (persons qualified as/who are/have been High Court Judges) to report sufficient cause before expiry of 3 months. Detained person must be told grounds "as soon as may be" and given earliest opportunity to represent (Art. 22(5)) — but facts against public interest need not be disclosed (Art. 22(6)).

Mnemonic: "24 hrs to magistrate, 3 months for Advisory Board" — two different clocks, don't confuse in MCQs.
Probable Q: Within how many hours must an arrested person be produced before a magistrate, and what time is excluded from this count?
Answer: 24 hours, excluding the journey time from the place of arrest to the magistrate's court.
🔴 Article 32 — Right to Constitutional Remedies Art. 32▶

Guarantees the right to move the Supreme Court directly for enforcement of Fundamental Rights. Dr. B.R. Ambedkar called it the "heart and soul of the Constitution."

WritMeaning / Purpose
Habeas Corpus"To have the body" — produce detained person before court; check illegal detention
Mandamus"We command" — directs public authority to perform a public/statutory duty
ProhibitionIssued by higher court to lower court/tribunal to stop exceeding jurisdiction
Quo Warranto"By what authority" — questions legality of a person holding a public office
Certiorari"To be certified" — quashes an order already passed by a lower court/tribunal for excess of jurisdiction/error

Art. 32(3) & (4)

Parliament may empower other courts with similar power within local limits (S.32(3)); the Art. 32 right itself cannot be suspended except as constitutionally provided (e.g., Emergency) (S.32(4)).

Mnemonic: "HMPQC" → Habeas corpus, Mandamus, Prohibition, Quo warranto, Certiorari. Say: "Ham Public ko Quick Certificate denge."
Probable Q: Which writ is used to challenge illegal detention of a person?
Answer: Habeas Corpus.
🔴 Articles 226 & 227 — High Court Writ & Superintendence Powers Art. 226-227▶

Article 226 🔴

Every High Court may issue the same 5 writs as Art. 32 — but for TWO purposes: (i) enforcement of Fundamental Rights (Part III), AND (ii) "any other purpose" (i.e., any legal right). This makes Art. 226 wider in scope than Art. 32, though Art. 32 remains a Fundamental Right itself while Art. 226 is a constitutional (not fundamental) right.

Art. 226(3): Interim Order Vacation 🟠

If a party affected by an ex-parte interim order applies for vacation (with copy served on the other side), the High Court must dispose of the vacation application within 2 weeks — else the interim order stands automatically vacated.

AspectArticle 32Article 226
CourtSupreme Court onlyConcerned High Court
PurposeOnly Fundamental RightsFundamental Rights + any other legal right
Nature of rightFundamental Right itselfConstitutional power (discretionary)
Can be suspendedYes, during Emergency (per Constitution)Not itself a Fundamental Right to suspend

Article 227 — Superintendence 🟠

Every High Court has power of superintendence over all courts and tribunals within its territorial jurisdiction — may call for returns, make rules for their practice/procedure, prescribe account-keeping forms, settle fee tables. Exception: does NOT extend to tribunals constituted under Armed Forces law (Art. 227(4)).

Probable Q: Why is the writ jurisdiction under Article 226 considered wider than under Article 32?
Answer: Because Art. 226 writs can be issued for enforcement of Fundamental Rights AND for "any other purpose" (any legal right), whereas Art. 32 is limited strictly to Fundamental Rights.
🔴 Article 311 — Protection to Civil Servants (MOST IMPORTANT for IP/AAO) Art. 311▶

Art. 311(1): Rule of Equal/Higher Authority 🔴

No member of a civil service of the Union/State/All-India Service, or holder of a civil post, shall be dismissed or removed by an authority subordinate to the one that appointed him.

Proposed dismissal / removal / reduction in rank
↓
MUST hold an Inquiry: inform charges + reasonable opportunity of being heard 🔴
↓ EXCEPT (Art. 311(2) proviso)
(a) Conviction on a criminal charge
(b) Not reasonably practicable to hold inquiry
(c) President/Governor satisfied — Security of the State

Direct Link to Natural Justice 🔴

Art. 311(2)'s "reasonable opportunity of being heard" is the constitutional codification of Audi Alteram Partem for civil servants — this is the single most direct connection between natural justice and the Constitution for IP/AAO exam purposes.

Where penalty is proposed after inquiry, it may be imposed based on evidence already on record — no separate opportunity needed on the penalty itself (post-42nd Amendment change). If a dispute arises whether holding an inquiry was "reasonably practicable," the decision of the dismissing authority is final (Art. 311(3)).

Mnemonic: "CPS" exceptions → Criminal conviction, Practicability issue, Security of State — no inquiry needed in these three.
Probable Q: What are the three circumstances under Article 311(2) where the inquiry requirement does not apply?
Answer: (a) Conviction on a criminal charge; (b) not reasonably practicable to hold inquiry; (c) security of the State considerations.

🕒 Key Constitutional Timeline महत्वपूर्ण समयरेखा
26 November 1949: Constitution adopted by Constituent Assembly (Preamble finalised)
26 January 1950: Constitution comes into force (Republic Day)
1963: 15th Amendment — restructured Art. 311(2) and (3)
1971: 24th Amendment — inserted Art. 13(4) (Parliament's amending power under Art. 368 not limited by Art. 13)
1976: 42nd Amendment — added "Socialist," "Secular" to Preamble; "integrity" to Fraternity; substituted Art. 226 (narrowed then later widened again); amended Art. 311(2) proviso (w.e.f. 3-1-1977)
1977–78: 43rd & 44th Amendments — restored/expanded Art. 226 writ scope, repealed Art. 226A/32A/228A (w.e.f. 13-4-1978, 1-8-1979)
2002: 86th Amendment — inserted Art. 21A (Right to Education)
Probable Q: Which amendment added "Socialist" and "Secular" to the Preamble, and which amendment inserted the Right to Education?
Answer: 42nd Amendment (1976) for Preamble words; 86th Amendment (2002) for Article 21A.
