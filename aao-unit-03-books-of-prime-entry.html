Golden Rules (learn cold)

  • Personal: Debit the receiver, Credit the giver
  • Real: Debit what comes in, Credit what goes out
  • Nominal: Debit expenses & losses, Credit incomes & gains

6 Source Documents

  • Cash receipt · Invoice · Debit note · Credit note · Pay-in-slip · Cheque

Accounting Equation

  • Capital + Liabilities = Assets
  • Expanded: Assets = Liabilities + Capital + Revenue − Expenses

Account Classification

  • Personal → Natural / Artificial / Representative
  • Impersonal → Real (Tangible/Intangible) & Nominal

📜 Important Rules

  • Golden Rules of Double Entry — the single most-tested table in this unit (see revision sheet).
  • For every debit, there must be an equivalent and corresponding credit.

⚖️ Important Exceptions

  • Purchases, Purchases Returns, Sales, Sales Returns are usually nominal accounts but may also be treated as real accounts since they relate to goods — common exam trap.

📅 Important Dates / Origins

  • "Dr." comes from Italian addebitare / Latin debere — has an 'r' despite "debit" not having one.
  • "Journal" derives from French Jour = day.

🏛️ Important Authorities / Definitions

  • Munro & Palmer — defined Double Entry Book-keeping.
  • J.R. Batliboi — defined Double Entry system.
  • Professor Carter — defined Journal.

⏱️ Important Time Limits

Journal entries are made daily / chronologically — no statutory time-limit; L.F. is filled only at time of posting.

🚫 Important Penalties

No penalty provision in this unit.

Accounting starts by identifying transactions and recording them based on authentic proofs, called source documents.

🟡 Conceptual Understanding Debit note = buyer's document (goods returned OUT). Credit note = seller's document (goods received back IN). Easy to reverse by mistake — remember "buyer debits, seller credits."
📝 Probable Question: Distinguish between debit note and credit note. (3 marks)
Munro & Palmer: every transaction has a two-fold aspect — receiving of value and giving of value — recorded via Double Entry Book-keeping.
J.R. Batliboi: every transaction affects two accounts in opposite directions — one debited, one credited.
Micro TopicKey Points
Principles (4)Two aspects in every transaction · receiving & giving aspects · minimum 2 accounts (1 debit + 1 credit) · every debit has equal corresponding credit
Advantages (4)Accuracy · ascertains business results · comparative study · common acceptance by institutions/govt.
🟢 Quick Revision Debit = increase in assets/expenses OR decrease in liabilities/income/capital. Credit = the exact opposite.

An account is the basic unit of identification in accounting — a systematic presentation of all information regarding a particular person/item at one place under one head.
📝 Probable Question: Define the term "account" in accounting. (1 mark)
Two approaches: (i) Accounting equation approach and (ii) Traditional approach (personal/real/nominal classification).

Decision Tree: Effect of a transaction on the equation
Same side, opposite effect (e.g. one asset up, another down)? → No net change in total
Opposite sides move together (e.g. asset up + capital up)? → Equation still balances
Profit earned on sale? → Capital increases by the profit amount
🟡 Conceptual Understanding The accounting equation NEVER goes out of balance — every transaction, however complex, keeps Assets = Liabilities + Capital true. This is the exam's favourite "fill in the missing figure" trick.

📝 Probable Question: State the golden rules of double entry accounting system with one example each. (6 marks — very high repeat rate)
Professor Carter: "The journal ... is a book of prime entry in which transactions are copied in order of date... classified into debits and credits, so as to facilitate correct posting to the ledger."
Journal = book of original / prime / primary / preliminary / first entry — transactions recorded in chronological order.

🟡 Conceptual Understanding Steps in journalising: identify accounts → classify (P/R/N) → apply golden rule → decide debit/credit → record with narration. This 5-step method solves any journal problem.

✓ Points to Remember (from textbook)

  • Transactions are recorded with the help of source documents.
  • Every transaction has debit and credit aspects — every debit has an equal, corresponding credit and vice versa.
  • Accounts are classified into personal, real and nominal.
  • Assets = Capital + Liabilities.
  • Transactions are first recorded in the journal.
